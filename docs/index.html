Martin is the exact kind of person that journalists would have no excuse to talk to.

He doesn't have a job. He doesn't have a wife or partner. He doesn't have children in his home. He's just a regular guy in his 60s who doesn't interact with that many people.

But we know a little bit about Martin because he recorded everything he did for one day in 2021.¹ And even with this small bit of information, there are potential red flags – because he could be missing something critical to human survival: friends, family, acquaintances – in short, social bonds.

Humans evolved to survive in groups, whether that's gathering food or fighting off predators. Without those connections, our bodies sound the alarm with pain.

"Feelings of loneliness told them when those protective bonds were endangered or deﬁcient," wrote neuroscientist John Cacioppo.

"What’s missing when you’re lonely is the feeling of closeness, trust, and the affection of genuine friends, loved ones, and community," writes former US surgeon general Vivek Murthy in his 2020 book, Together. "When we feel lonely, we’re unhappy and long to escape this emotional pain."

We're no longer hunted by predators, but strong relationships are still a fundamental human need. Without those bonds, we feel more anxious, more fearful, and more stressed. Because thousands of years of evolution made us believe that when we're isolated, we're in danger.

The average human knows about 5,000 faces. We can put a name to about 1,500 of them, and we personally know about 500.¹ But most of those people won't help us in times of need, nor will they make us feel less isolated.

The people who will help are our friends – and psychologist Robin Dunbar argues that we have an average of 150 people who we try to contact at least once a year.² Within that group, he found that we have 50 people who contact once every six month; 15 people we contact once a month; five people we contact once a week; and one to two people with whom we share "really intense relationships like romantic partners." (Often, women also have this kind of relationship with their "Best Friend Forever.")

Lonely people report feeling more shy, more anxious, more negative, and more angry. They are less optimistic and have lower self esteem.

Are they inherently different compared to everyone else?

In a 2006 study, neuroscientist John Cacioppo and his colleagues hypnotized a bunch of college students to feel lonely. They told the subjects: “Think of a time in which you felt isolated. You felt lonely. Perhaps you felt like you just didn’t belong – that you had no friends.”

After confirming the hypnotism worked, they found that the students who were made to be lonely reported the exact same negative feelings as people who were actually lonely. It confirmed some of his previous findings: Loneliness can find all of us.

"We had demonstrated yet again that lonely individuals are not a breed apart," Cacioppo wrote in his book, Loneliness. "Any of us can succumb to loneliness, and along with it, all the other characteristics that travel as its entourage."

Loneliness robs you of your best self.

Research has shown that isolated people are less generous with donations, less likely to persevere in the face of adversity, more likely to procrastinate, and more likely to administer harsher punishments.¹ Lonely people get lower quality sleep. And it may predict the progression of Alzheimer's disease.

Ultimately, lonely people are 50% more likely to die prematurely than people with strong social connections – the equivalent of having six alcoholic drinks a day or smoking 15 cigarettes a day.

¹ From Cacioppo's book, Loneliness

When we see people cry out for help, it often looks like they are unable to manage their emotions. Sometimes we assume they are unpleasant people who don't deserve company. We ostracize them.

So when we are lonely, we hide it.

We crawl deeper into our shells because we feel weak and vulnerable. When we're lonely, we become even more wary of social threats, so the very thing we need – social interaction – feels dangerous. We start to blame ourselves; we wonder if something really is wrong with us.

"Over time," Murthy writes, "this vicious cycle may convince us we don’t matter to anyone and that we’re unworthy of love."

Experts have said we are experiencing an "epidemic of loneliness."

But I'm worried that this epidemic is invisible.

We don't share that we are lonely. We treat it like an individual problem, not a structural one. After all, if people don't have what they need – food, shelter, money – we often tell them it's their own damn fault.

Yet more and more people are feeling the pain of isolation. Sure, you could say more and more people are at fault. But what if we've created a world that tells people they aren't worthy of love?
